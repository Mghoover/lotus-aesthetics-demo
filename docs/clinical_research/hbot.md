Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT)
Scientific overview, evidence positioning, safety considerations, and service-page guidance
Positioning note: HBOT has well-established uses in wound care, decompression illness, carbon monoxide poisoning, radiation injury, and several other recognized indications. Neurologic and cognitive applications remain an active research area. For brain-health marketing, the page should be aspirational but medically conservative, and it should not describe EEG findings as stand-alone proof of benefit.
1. Introduction
Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBOT) is a treatment in which a patient breathes near-100% oxygen inside a pressurized chamber. Clinical HBOT is generally defined as treatment at pressures of at least 1.4 atmospheres absolute (ATA), producing oxygen tensions in blood and tissue that are not achievable by breathing oxygen at normal atmospheric pressure alone.
HBOT is an established part of care for a defined set of medical indications, and it is also being actively studied for additional uses involving neurology, rehabilitation, and recovery. Because clinics often market HBOT broadly, it is important to distinguish between indications with broad acceptance and areas where the evidence is still emerging.
2. Established clinical uses versus emerging uses
The Undersea and Hyperbaric Medical Society (UHMS) lists recognized indications such as air or gas embolism, carbon monoxide poisoning, gas gangrene, crush injury and other acute traumatic ischemias, decompression sickness, selected problem wounds, severe anemia, intracranial abscess, necrotizing soft tissue infection, refractory osteomyelitis, delayed radiation injury, compromised grafts and flaps, sudden sensorineural hearing loss, and thermal burns.
Coverage policy in the United States is similarly selective. For example, CMS covers HBOT for specified conditions and, for diabetic lower-extremity wounds, only as adjunctive therapy after an adequate period of standard wound care without measurable healing. This matters for website positioning: the strongest claims should be attached to recognized indications, while neurologic or performance-oriented claims should be framed as investigational, emerging, or condition-specific.
3. Core biological mechanisms
HBOT increases dissolved oxygen in plasma and can temporarily raise tissue oxygen levels well beyond what is possible under normal atmospheric conditions. Proposed mechanisms include improved oxygen delivery to hypoxic tissue, support for angiogenesis and fibroblast activity, modulation of inflammation, effects on leukocyte function and host defense, and changes in oxidative signaling that may alter repair pathways.
In neurologic literature, HBOT has also been discussed as a possible neuromodulatory or neuroplasticity-supporting intervention. Reviews describe potential effects on mitochondrial function, neuroinflammation, cerebral blood flow, and cellular signaling. These mechanisms are biologically plausible, but mechanism plausibility alone does not establish clinical effectiveness for every marketed indication.
4. Brain and cognitive applications: where the evidence stands
HBOT is increasingly discussed in relation to concussion, traumatic brain injury (TBI), post-concussion symptoms, PTSD, cognitive recovery, and broader “brain optimization” narratives. This is the area where the clinic’s future service page needs the most discipline.
A 2025 information paper from the Defense Health Agency’s Traumatic Brain Injury Center of Excellence concluded that evidence does not currently support HBOT as an evidence-based therapy for TBI or post-concussion symptoms in military or civilian populations. That paper notes that several better-controlled military studies found no significant advantage over sham treatment, and the 2021 VA/DoD guideline recommends against HBOT for symptoms attributed to mild TBI. The same paper also notes that some studies report improvements in cognition, pain, blood-flow markers, or symptoms, but these positive findings are often limited by methodological issues, small samples, lack of appropriate controls, or potential conflicts of interest.
The fairest scientific position is that neurologic HBOT remains an active research area with mixed results. It should not be marketed as settled science for concussion, PTSD, or generalized brain health. A responsible clinic page can say that HBOT is being studied for certain neurologic applications and that clinical benefit may depend heavily on indication, protocol, patient selection, and study design.
5. EEG and “proof of results”
Because your planned installation includes EEG, it is worth drawing a careful boundary between “objective monitoring” and “proof.” EEG can be a useful physiologic tool for baseline characterization, task-based comparison, or longitudinal monitoring in selected neurologic workflows. A recent trial sub-study design in veterans with TBI specifically describes EEG as a promising diagnostic and therapeutic-monitoring measure within HBOT research.
However, that publication is a study protocol, not final proof of clinical efficacy. More broadly, EEG changes do not automatically translate into durable patient-important benefit such as better function, fewer symptoms, or improved quality of life. For a clinic website, the safe and accurate wording is that EEG may help document patterns of brain activity before, during, or after a care plan in selected patients. It should not be represented as universal proof that HBOT has “worked,” nor as a validated biomarker of success across all indications.
6. Safety and operational realities
HBOT is generally considered safe when performed in an appropriate facility by trained personnel using cleared equipment and strict protocols. Even so, it is not risk-free. Commonly discussed adverse effects include ear or sinus barotrauma, claustrophobia, temporary vision changes such as transient myopia, and fatigue. More serious complications can include oxygen toxicity, pneumothorax, and rare severe events.
In August 2025, the FDA issued a letter to health care providers reminding facilities to follow manufacturer instructions, maintain fire prevention measures, provide adequate staff training, and continuously monitor patients. The FDA noted reports of serious injuries and deaths associated with HBOT devices, including fire-related events. For this reason, any future service page should emphasize qualified supervision, patient screening, and adherence to facility safety protocols rather than presenting HBOT as a spa-style wellness experience.
7. How the service page should be positioned
For this clinic, HBOT is best positioned as a high-acuity, evidence-aware therapy entering the practice with a strong emphasis on safety, patient selection, and objective monitoring. The page should avoid broad promises such as “repairs the brain,” “reverses neurologic damage,” or “proves improvement in real time.” It should also avoid implying FDA clearance or guideline endorsement for indications that remain investigational.
A strong patient-facing page can instead say that HBOT is a pressurized oxygen therapy with well-established medical uses and growing research interest in recovery-oriented and neurologic applications. It can explain that some patients may also undergo EEG-based assessments as part of a broader evaluation and monitoring process. The call to action should invite consultation and screening, not promise guaranteed outcomes.

Website-safe language checklist
•	Allowed framing: “HBOT is an established medical therapy for selected indications and is also being studied in additional neurologic applications.”
•	Allowed framing: “EEG may be used as part of baseline and follow-up assessment in selected patients.”
•	Allowed framing: “Clinical suitability depends on indication, history, screening, and physician oversight.”
•	Avoid: “HBOT is proven for brain optimization,” “EEG proves results,” “HBOT reverses brain injury,” or “FDA-approved for concussion/PTSD.”
